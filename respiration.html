Glycolysis

The initial, anaerobic splitting of a 6-carbon glucose molecule into two 3-carbon pyruvate molecules.

Key Inputs
  • 1x Glucose (6C)
  • 2x ATP (Activation energy)
  • 2x NAD+
Key Outputs
  • 2x Pyruvate (3C)
  • 4x ATP (Net yield: 2 ATP)
  • 2x NADH
Biological Significance

Glycolysis occurs universally in almost all living cells. Because it does not require oxygen, it is the fundamental energy-yielding pathway in anaerobic environments and the necessary precursor for aerobic respiration. It involves an initial energy investment phase followed by an energy payoff phase.

ℹ
Higher Biology Focus

Note that glycolysis occurs in the cytoplasm, not the mitochondria. Dehydrogenase enzymes are critical here, removing hydrogen ions and electrons to reduce NAD+ to NADH, an essential carrier for the final stage.

Transport

Pyruvate produced in the cytoplasm during glycolysis is actively transported across the inner mitochondrial membrane and into the mitochondrial matrix.

NADH → NAD⁺ FADH₂ → FAD
½ O₂ + 2H⁺ → H₂O
ADP + Pi → ATP

1. Electron Delivery

NADH deposits high-energy electrons at Complex I (NADH dehydrogenase). FADH₂ bypasses Complex I and deposits electrons slightly downstream at Complex II (Succinate dehydrogenase).

Key Components Active

Complex I, Complex II

Incomplete Breakdown

Without oxygen, the ETC halts. NAD+ cannot be regenerated from NADH aerobically. Fermentation occurs to recycle NAD+ so glycolysis can continue, providing a minimal ATP lifeline.

Animal Cells (Reversible): Pyruvate is reduced to Lactate. Causes muscle fatigue.
Plant/Yeast Cells (Irreversible): Pyruvate converted to Ethanol and CO2.
