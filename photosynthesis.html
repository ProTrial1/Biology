The Two Stages of Photosynthesis

Photosynthesis is not a single event, but a complex series of biochemical pathways split into two main stages. Compare them below to see how light energy is ultimately stored as chemical energy.

Capturing the Sun

These reactions occur in the thylakoid membrane. They require direct sunlight to operate.

  • 📥 Inputs: Light Energy, H₂O, NADP+, ADP
  • ⚙️ Process: Chlorophyll absorbs light, exciting electrons. Water is split (photolysis) to replace electrons, releasing oxygen. Energy creates an ATP and NADPH gradient.
  • 📤 Outputs: ATP, NADPH, O₂ (Byproduct)
💧 ➔ ⚡ + 💨

Water enters, Light powers it up, Oxygen leaves, Energy carriers (ATP/NADPH) are charged.

Building the Sugar

Occurring in the stroma, this cycle uses the energy harvested in the first stage to fix carbon. It does not strictly require light, but depends on the products of the light reactions.

  • 📥 Inputs: CO₂, ATP, NADPH
  • ⚙️ Process: Carbon Fixation (catalyzed by Rubisco enzyme), Reduction (using ATP/NADPH), and Regeneration of RuBP to keep the cycle turning.
  • 📤 Outputs: G3P (Sugar precursor), ADP, NADP+
☁️ + ⚡ ➔ 🍬

Carbon Dioxide enters, stored Energy is spent, Sugar molecules are built.

Carbon Fixation

The cycle begins when carbon dioxide (CO₂) from the air enters the stroma. An enzyme named Rubisco (the most abundant protein on Earth!) attaches a 1-carbon CO₂ molecule to a 5-carbon starting molecule called RuBP.

The Chemistry

3 CO₂ + 3 RuBP ➔ 6 3-PGA
(The resulting 6-carbon molecule is so unstable it immediately splits into two 3-carbon molecules called 3-PGA).

Limiting Factors

The rate of photosynthesis isn't constant. It is constrained by whichever necessary factor is in shortest supply. Use the controls to visualize how Light, Carbon Dioxide, and Temperature affect the production rate.

Light Intensity: As light increases, the rate of photosynthesis increases linearly until a certain point. It eventually plateaus because another factor (like CO2 or temperature) becomes the new limiting factor, or chlorophyll is saturated.

Evolutionary Adaptations: C3, C4, and CAM

The biggest threat to a plant in a hot climate is Photorespiration—a wasteful glitch where Rubisco accidentally binds to Oxygen instead of CO₂, wasting energy and carbon. Over millions of years, plants evolved ingenious physical and chemical workarounds to survive different climates.

~85% of all plant species

C3 Photosynthesis

This is the default, ancestral form of photosynthesis. It is called "C3" because the first stable carbon compound produced in the Calvin Cycle is a 3-carbon molecule (3-PGA).

✅ The Mechanism

CO₂ enters directly into the mesophyll cells where the Calvin cycle occurs. It relies entirely on the enzyme Rubisco to fix carbon.

⚠️ The Flaw

In hot, dry weather, stomata close to prevent water loss. O₂ builds up, CO₂ drops, and Rubisco mistakenly binds to O₂. This triggers photorespiration, draining the plant's energy.

Ideal Climate: Cool, moist, and temperate environments.
Common Examples: Wheat, rice, soybeans, most trees, and lawn grasses.
